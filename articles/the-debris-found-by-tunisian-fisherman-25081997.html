Photographs of the debris supposedly found by Tunisian fisherman in the Gulf Of Gebas

The following is an explanation of the photographs taken by Cormac Boomer on 25th September 1997.

Note the volume of debris recovered is very small and specific given the overall size of the plane.

The remains of the instrument panel

The steering column with the right arm missing. The rudder pedals and the control handles for the engine ailerons and flaps One of the seatbelts recovered

Close up of the bottom of the steering column

Accumulation of rust suggests this was out of the water and exposed to atmosphere for a long period of time. Also shown with the name Piper and the control handles

Control cable and linkage from around the steering column

One end of the stainless steel cable is frayed and bent the investigator stated this condition is the result of being stretched beyond breaking point. The other end suggests a twisted wrung off break, no explanation why the two ends should be different

The landing gear leaver handle

This is in the first notch position. This would indicate the landing gear was partially lowered. Also depicted are two seat belts

Closeup of the rudder pedal

A closeup of the rudder pedal and the bottom of the steering column

Seat bolts ripped from the floor

One of the two seat bolts and securing fixtures which have been ripped from the floor. Note the securing bolts are severely bent. The material of these belts was stretched (elongated) beyond belief. Note the release leaver is compressed into and on top of the retaining mechanism. The name and number plate of the manufacturer is also visible

Aileron Servo Motor with manufacturers aluminium name plate

Note the manufacturers aluminium name plate which shows no signs of salt pigment or corrosion. We are asked to believe this item was eight months in the salt water of the Mediterranean sea

The Auto Pilot Motor

Note the very good condition ofthe manufacturers name plate

Main spar with floor carpet

This is part of the main spar with the floor carpet in place. This spar is the component across the body of the aircraft to which the wing sections are attached. It is positioned directly behind the pilot/co-pilot’s seats

Spar with the floor carpet in normal position

Registration Number 8040

The carpet pulled back revels the number 80410. This is the registration number allocated to this aircraft during manufacturer. This is inscribed using an indelible marker on this component

Frames securing the pilots seats

This depicts the frames upon which the pilots seats are secured. Note the retaining brackets have been ripped off. There is no indication of stress visible on the adjoining cable looms

Frames securing the pilots seats

This depicts the frames upon which the pilots seats are secured. Note the retaining brackets have been ripped off There is no indication of stress visible on the adjoining cable looms

Two pieces of fuselage skin

These are two pieces of fuselage skin from which Mr. Lehman calculated the approximate angle of entry of the aircraft into the sea. It is important to record here these items are totally different to the parts of fuselage depicted in the photographic evidence submitted by the court in November 1996. The Court of Enquiry refused to make available to me (Cormac Boomer), even when the request was delivered through the Dept. of Foreign Affairs in Dublin

Indicator light bulb from intrument panel

This is the only surviving item from the pilot’s instrument panel. It is an indictor light bulb 2x1cm and is in perfect working order. Mr Lehman explained this is an indicator light for the right wing tip navigation light. He explained that had this light been on when the aircraft hit the water this would have caused a short in the filament of the bulb. The condition of the bulb suggests the aircraft was flying without navigation lights in the on position

Section of floor to rear of pilot seat

These depict sections of the floor area to the rear of the pilot’s seat

Section of floor to rear of pilot seat

Sections of the floor area to the rear of the pilot’s seat

Close up view photograph 16/17

This is a close-up view of 16 & 17. Note the cable ends appear to be snipped off at the same point. There is no sign of fray, stretch, or the rupture one would likely expect to find in an aircraft ripped apart by plunging in to the sea from 9000 feet
