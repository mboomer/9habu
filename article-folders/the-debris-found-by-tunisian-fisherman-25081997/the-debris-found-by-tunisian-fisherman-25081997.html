Photographs of the debris supposedly found by Tunisian fisherman in the Gulf Of Gebas

The following is an explanation of the photographs taken by Cormac Boomer on 25th September 1997.

Note the volume of debris recovered is very small and specific given the overall size of the plane.

  This photograph shows

  • The remains of the instrument panel
  • The steering column with the right arm missing
  • The rudder pedals and the control handles for the engine ailerons and flaps
  • One of the seatbelts recovered

  This photograph shows

  • Close up of the bottom of the steering column
  • Accumulation of rust suggests this was out of the water and exposed to atmosphere for a long period of time
  • Also shown with the name Piper and the control handles

  This photograph shows

  • This is control cable and linkage from around the steering column
  • Note that one end of the stainless steel cable is frayed and bent
  • the investigator stated this condition is the result of being stretched beyond breaking point
  • The other end suggests a twisted wrung off break, no explanation why the two ends should be different

  This photograph shows

  • The landing gear leaver handle
  • This is in the first notch position
  • This would indicate the landing gear was partially lowered
  • Also depicted are two seat belts

  This photograph shows

  • A closeup of the rudder pedal and the bottom of the steering column

  This photograph shows

  • One of the two seat bolts and securing fixtures which have been ripped from the floor
  • Note the securing bolts are severely bent
  • The material of these belts was stretched (elongated) beyond belief
  • Note the release leaver is compressed into and on top of the retaining mechanism
  • the name and number plate of the manufacturer is also visible

  This photograph shows

  • The Aileron Servo Motor
  • Note the manufacturers aluminium name plate which shows no signs of salt pigment or corrosion
  • We are asked to believe this item was eight months in the salt water of the Mediterranean sea

  This photograph shows

  • The Auto Pilot Motor
  • Note the very good condition ofthe manufacturers name plate

  This photograph shows

  • This is part of the main spar with the floor carpet in place
  • This spar is the component across the body of the aircraft to which the wing sections are attached
  • It is positioned directly behind the pilot/co-pilot’s seats

  This photograph shows

  • The spar with the floor carpet in normal position

  This photograph shows

  • The carpet pulled back revels the number 80410
  • This is the registration number allocated to this aircraft during manufacturer
  • This is inscribed using an indelible marker on this component

  This photograph shows

  • This depicts the frames upon which the pilots seats are secured
  • Note the retaining brackets have been ripped off
  • There is no indication of stress visible on the adjoining cable looms

  This photograph shows

  • This depicts the frames upon which the pilots seats are secured
  • Note the retaining brackets have been ripped off
  • There is no indication of stress visible on the adjoining cable looms

  This photograph shows

  • These are two pieces of fuselage skin from which Mr. Lehman calculated the approximate angle of entry of the aircraft into the sea
  • It is important to record here these items are totally different to the parts of fuselage depicted in the photographic evidence submitted by the court in November 1996
  • The Court of Enquiry refused to make available to me (Cormac Boomer), even when the request was delivered through the Dept. of Foreign Affairs in Dublin

  This photograph shows

  • This is the only surviving item from the pilot’s instrument panel
  • It is an indictor light bulb 2x1cm and is in perfect working order
  • Mr Lehman explained this is an indicator light for the right wing tip navigation light
  • He explained that had this light been on when the aircraft hit the water this would have caused a short in the filament of the bulb
  • The condition of the bulb suggests the aircraft was flying without navigation lights in the on position
